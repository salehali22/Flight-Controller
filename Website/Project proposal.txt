1. Introduction 
 
1.1 Background and societal Need 
The growing integration of autonomous and semi-autonomous systems across industrial, 
commercial, and research sectors has elevated the importance of advanced embedded control 
platforms. Modern applications in infrastructure inspection, environmental monitoring, public 
safety, logistics, agriculture, and robotics rely on precise, real-time sensor processing and stable 
control algorithms to ensure safe and efficient operation. As industries accelerate toward 
automation, the demand for engineers capable of designing, analyzing, and implementing 
control systems at the hardware and firmware levels continues to increase. 
Many existing flight-control solutions, while mature, are designed around fixed configurations or 
proprietary architectures that limit deep system customization, scalability, or adaptation to 
specialized mission profiles. Industrial applications often require hardware capable of 
supporting heterogeneous sensors, redundant configurations, and flexible communication 
interfaces to accommodate varying operational standards and safety requirements. 
Furthermore, as autonomous systems evolve, industries increasingly favor platforms that offer 
transparency, long-term maintainability, and the ability to integrate custom logic or specialized 
control algorithms without dependence on closed ecosystems. 
These trends highlight a clear need for modular, adaptable, and technically open flight-control 
architectures that can be tailored to different UAV platforms and operational scenarios. A 
custom controller designed with modern performance requirements, robust interfacing 
capabilities, and compatibility with established firmware ecosystems directly addresses this gap, 
supporting both current industry practices and anticipated future developments in autonomous 
aerial systems. 
1.2 Project overview and Objective 
This project proposes the design and development of a fully custom flight-controller platform 
intended for use across multiple unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) configurations. The controller is 
built around a high-performance STM32H743 microcontroller operating at 480 MHz, selected 
for its computational capacity, extensive peripheral set, and suitability for real-time control 
tasks. The hardware integrates a complete suite of essential flight sensors, including inertial 
measurement units, barometric pressure sensing, magnetometers, and satellite-based 
positioning modules. These components enable accurate attitude estimation, altitude 
determination, heading reference, and global navigation, forming the sensor backbone required 
for stable flight and autonomous operation.

A key design goal is modularity. The board incorporates standard communication interfaces 
allowing integration with diverse UAV frames, ESC systems, GPS receivers, radio-control 
protocols, and auxiliary sensors. The hardware architecture is engineered to maintain 
compatibility with leading open-source firmware ecosystems such as ArduPilot, iNav, and 
Betaflight, ensuring interoperability while also enabling the development of custom firmware 
tailored to advanced applications. This dual-purpose approach supports both rapid validations 
using established software and deeper experimentation through original embedded-systems 
development. 
Physical design adheres to practical constraints, targeting a compact form factor suitable for 50 
mm flight-controller mounting standards commonly used in quadcopters and adaptable to 
fixed-wing VTOL platforms. The project includes full PCB schematic design, signal-integrity 
considerations, component selection, power-distribution architecture, and manufacturing 
through a budget-conscious prototyping process. By June, the team will demonstrate stable 
manual and autonomous quadcopter flight as proof of concept, validating both the hardware 
platform and the foundational firmware components. 
The project showcases a complete engineering workflow from concept and design to 
implementation and testing while delivering a functional controller that is cost-efficient, 
educationally valuable, and aligned with emerging industry needs for adaptable autonomous-
system hardware. 
 
1.3 Project Objectives 
The objective of this project is to design and implement a custom, modular flight-controller 
hardware platform that can support a broad range of UAV firmware ecosystems and vehicle 
configurations. The primary focus is the development of robust electronic architecture; 
firmware development is limited to essential board validation and flight testing. 
The project aims to achieve the following: 
1. Develop a complete custom PCB flight controller, integrating a high-performance STM32 
microcontroller, essential flight sensors, GNSS support, and a reliable power-distribution 
and protection system. 
2. Implement a modular hardware interface layout, providing standardized UART, SPI, I²C, 
PWM, USB, and power connections to ensure compatibility with diverse sensors, ESCs, 
radio systems, and potential future expansions.

3. Align the hardware design with established open-source firmware ecosystems, enabling 
the board to operate with ArduPilot, iNav, Betaflight, or other platforms without 
requiring significant architectural changes. 
4. Perform minimal required firmware bring-up, limited to sensor communication tests,

1.4 Project Scope 
This project encompasses the design and implementation of a custom, modular flight-controller 
hardware platform together with the development of essential flight-control algorithms 
required for stable manual flight and basic autonomous operation. The scope includes complete 
PCB and hardware architecture design, sensor integration, power-management circuitry, and 
communication-interface layout. On the firmware side, the project will implement core 
stabilization logic, cascaded PID control loops, sensor fusion for attitude estimation, and simple 
autonomous behaviors such as basic waypoint navigation or position hold. Advanced mission 
planning, complex autonomy, and large-scale firmware ecosystems remain outside the project’s 
scope, as the primary objective is to deliver a functional and extensible hardware platform 
validated through controlled, stable flight. 
1.5 Limitations 
Autonomous functionality is restricted to basic behaviors intended for validation purposes 
rather than operational deployment. Environmental robustness, regulatory certification, and 
extended reliability testing fall outside the scope of this work and are assumed to be addressed 
in future iterations. It is further assumed that standard UAV components such as motors, ESCs, 
batteries, and radio-control systems operate within nominal specifications and are compatible 
with the designed hardware platform. Moreover, the initial design will be based on industrial 
standards and will follow codes and protocols that take certification requirements from (EASA , 
FAA) into account.

2. Technical Approach and Methodology 
 
 
Figure [1] 
2.1 Overall System Architecture 
The technical approach of this project is centered on the development of modular, hardware-
first flight-controller architecture capable of supporting multiple UAV configurations and 
firmware ecosystems. The system is organized into four primary subsystems: power 
management, processing and control, sensing and peripheral integration, and external 
connectivity. A block-level representation of the complete architecture is shown in Figure 1, 
illustrating the relationship between the microcontroller core and the surrounding hardware 
modules. 
At the center of the system is a high-performance STM32H743 microcontroller, selected for its 
real-time processing capability, extensive peripheral support, and suitability for flight-control 
applications. Surrounding the MCU, dedicated power-conditioning circuitry ensures stable 
operation under electrically noisy UAV environments, while multiple sensor interfaces and 
communication ports enable flexible integration with external components such as ESCs, GPS 
modules, radio receivers, and telemetry systems.

The architecture prioritizes hardware modularity and electrical robustness. Standardized 
interfaces (SPI, I²C, UART, PWM, CAN, USB) are exposed through dedicated connectors, allowing 
the flight controller to operate with different firmware platforms and airframe types without 
requiring hardware redesign. Firmware development is treated as a validation layer, used 
primarily to confirm sensor communication, control-loop execution, and overall system stability. 
2.2 Power Management System 
The power-management and protection subsystem is designed to ensure stable and reliable 
operation under the electrical conditions typical of UAV platforms, including voltage 
fluctuations, high-current transients, and electromagnetic interference generated by motors 
and ESCs. Reverse-polarity protection, transient voltage suppression, and input filtering are 
implemented at the power entry to protect against incorrect connection and fast electrical 
disturbances. It is worth noting that there will be a dedicated power distribution board for the 
motors, servos and power demanding modules. Therefore, expanding the compatibility borders 
and reinforcing the separation of low-power and high-power systems.  
Voltage regulation is achieved through high-efficiency buck converters generating intermediate 
supply rails, followed by dedicated low-dropout regulators supplying isolated 3.3 V domains for 
digital logic and inertial sensors. This staged regulation approach reduces noise coupling 
between high-current digital loads and noise-sensitive sensing circuitry. Voltage and current 
monitoring are integrated via an I²C-connected sensing device to support diagnostics and 
power-awareness. A USB-C power interface with automatic source selection is included to 
support development and testing without reliance on the main battery.  
2.2.1 Design Standards and Protocols 
The power subsystem is designed in accordance with established industrial best practices and 
standards. Electromagnetic immunity and transient robustness follow the principles of the IEC 
61000 series. Low-voltage safety considerations are guided by IEC 62368-1. PCB layout and 
power-distribution practices adhere to IPC-2221 and IPC-9592 recommendations, while USB-C 
power behavior aligns with the USB Type-C specification. These standards are applied as design 
references rather than certification targets.

2.3  Processing and Control Core 
The processing and control core of the flight controller is based on the STM32H743 
microcontroller, featuring an ARM Cortex-M7 core operating at up to 480 MHz with single-
precision floating-point support (FPU). This processing capability is sufficient to execute real-
time attitude estimation, cascaded PID control loops, and peripheral communication within 
deterministic timing constraints typical of UAV flight-control systems. 
The microcontroller provides 1 MB of on-chip RAM and 2 MB of Flash memory, allowing 
separation of program memory, runtime data, and DMA buffers. Multiple Advanced Control 
Timers (TIM1/TIM8) and general-purpose timers support generation of motor and servo control 
signals with microsecond-level resolution, enabling precise PWM outputs for multirotor and 
fixed-wing actuation. Timer allocation is selected to maintain independent control of motor 
outputs and auxiliary actuators. 
The clock system uses an external high-speed crystal oscillator (HSE) as the primary reference 
source to ensure stable timing for control loops and communication peripherals. The internal 
PLL is configured to achieve the target core frequency while maintaining peripheral clock 
domains within their specified limits. This clocking approach improves timing determinism 
compared to internal RC oscillators, particularly for high-speed SPI communication and control-
loop scheduling. 
Power is supplied to the MCU through multiple dedicated supply pins (VDD, VDDA, VDDUSB), 
each locally decoupled using a combination of bulk capacitors (µF range) and high-frequency 
ceramic capacitors (100nF and below). Analog and digital supply domains are treated separately 
at the schematic level to reduce coupling of digital switching noise into ADCs and other noise-
sensitive circuits. 
The STM32H743 provides multiple SPI, I²C, UART/USART, ADC, USB 2.0 FS, and CAN-FD capable 
interfaces, enabling clean separation of sensor, control, and communication traffic. High-
bandwidth sensors such as IMUs are connected via SPI, while lower-speed peripherals are 
assigned to I²C or UART interfaces. Direct Memory Access (DMA) is employed for SPI and UART 
data transfers to reduce CPU load and ensure predictable execution of time-critical control 
tasks. 
Pin multiplexing and peripheral mapping are selected to avoid resource conflicts and to 
maintain compatibility with established open-source firmware platforms. Debug and 
programming access is provided through SWD, allowing low-level firmware development, 
register-level debugging, and performance analysis during system bring-up and validation.

2.3.1 Design Standards and Protocols 
The processing and control core is designed following established embedded-system and PCB 
design practices addressing signal integrity, electromagnetic compatibility, and timing stability. 
Clock distribution, power decoupling, and grounding strategies are implemented in accordance 
with IPC-2221 recommendations and EMC-oriented design principles derived from the IEC 
61000 framework. USB connectivity associated with the microcontroller follows the USB 2.0 
electrical specification to ensure reliable communication and signal integrity 
 
2.4 Sensors and Peripherals 
The sensor and peripheral subsystem are responsible for acquiring all primary state variables 
required for flight stabilization, navigation, and pilot feedback. The design integrates both 
onboard sensors and standardized external interfaces to support multirotor, fixed-wing, and 
VTOL configurations while maintaining compatibility with existing UAV firmware ecosystems.  
2.4.1 Inertial Measurement Units (IMUs) 
The flight controller integrates two independent 6-axis inertial measurement units: 
• Primary IMU: InvenSense ICM-42688-P 
• Secondary IMU: Bosch BMI270 
Both devices provide tri-axis accelerometer and gyroscope measurements and operate from a 
3.3 V ±5% supply. The IMUs are interfaced via SPI, selected over I²C to meet deterministic 
latency and bandwidth requirements for control loops operating at ≥1 kHz. 
Each IMU is connected to the MCU via: 
• Dedicated chip-select (CS) line 
• Shared SPI clock (SCK), MOSI, MISO 
The SPI bus is configured to support clock rates up to 8–10 MHz, well within the electrical limits 
of both devices, ensuring sufficient bandwidth for: 
• Gyroscope output data rates up to 4 kHz (ICM-42688-P) 
• Accelerometer output data rates up to 1.6 kHz

This configuration enables raw inertial data acquisition with sub-millisecond latency. Redundant 
IMU integration allows: 
• Sensor cross-validation 
• Firmware-level failover 
• Improved robustness under vibration and transient disturbances 
2.4.2 Barometric and Air-Pressure Sensing 
Altitude estimation is provided by an onboard BMP388 barometric pressure sensor, interfaced 
via I²C. The sensor operates from a 3.3 V supply and supports pressure resolution on the order 
of ±0.08 m under nominal conditions, sufficient for altitude-hold control and vertical velocity 
estimation. 
The I²C bus operates at 400 kHz (Fast mode), balancing update rate and bus stability. Pull-up 
resistors are selected to meet I²C rise-time requirements for the expected bus capacitance, 
including external devices. 
In addition to onboard barometric sensing, a dedicated external airspeed/pressure connector is 
provided for differential pressure sensors. This interface supplies: 
• Regulated 3.3 V power 
• I²C communication 
This enables air speed measurement for fixed-wing and VTOL platforms without additional 
analog front-end circuitry on the flight controller. 
2.4.3 GPS and Magnetometer Interface 
Global position and heading reference are provided through a combined GPS and 
magnetometer module connected via a dedicated external connector. The interface supplies 
regulated power and digital communication lines required for position, velocity, and heading 
estimation. 
The GPS receiver communicates with the flight controller through a UART interface operating at 
3.3 V logic levels, supporting baud rates up to 115200 bps. This bandwidth is sufficient for 
standard GPS message formats (e.g., NMEA sentences or vendor-specific binary protocols) at 
update rates commonly ranging from 1 Hz to 10 Hz. The UART interface is electrically isolated 
from high-bandwidth sensor buses to prevent interference with time-critical inertial data 
acquisition.

The integrated magnetometer within the GPS module is interfaced via I²C, operating at 3.3 V 
and configured for 400 kHz fast-mode operation. Locating the magnetometer within an external 
GPS module allows physical separation from high-current conductors, voltage regulators, and 
switching elements on the flight controller, reducing susceptibility to magnetic interference 
without requiring onboard shielding or compensation circuitry. 
The GPS/magnetometer connector provides: 
• Regulated supply voltage (5V) 
• UART TX/RX lines for GPS data 
• I²C SCL/SDA lines for magnetometer communication 
• Ground reference 
This connector-level definition enables direct compatibility with widely used UAV GPS–compass 
modules and allows the same flight controller hardware to be deployed across different 
airframes without modification. 
2.4.4 On-Screen Display and Digital Video Systems 
An onboard AT7456E OSD controller is interfaced via SPI to support real-time telemetry overlay 
on analog video signals. The SPI interface is operated within the device’s specified clock limits to 
ensure stable character rendering and video synchronization. 
In parallel, the flight controller includes a dedicated connector for DJI Air Unit–class digital video 
systems, providing: 
• Regulated power rails (9 V / 12 V, as required) 
• UART communication for telemetry and configuration 
• Control signaling compatible with DJI digital FPV ecosystems 
Electrical pin assignments and voltage levels are selected to match DJI Air Unit interface 
requirements, enabling direct integration without external adapters. 
 
2.4.5 CAN Bus Interface and Peripheral Support 
The flight controller integrates a dedicated CAN transceiver connected to the MCU’s FDCAN 
peripheral, enabling robust differential communication with external UAV subsystems. The CAN 
interface is designed to support distributed peripherals such as smart ESCs, power modules, 
range sensors, or companion control boards commonly used in modern UAV architectures.

The CAN transceiver operates at 3.3 V logic levels on the MCU side and interfaces to a 
differential CAN bus (CANH/CANL) on the external side. The design supports standard CAN 
physical-layer requirements, including differential signaling and common-mode noise rejection, 
making it suitable for electrically noisy environments typical of propulsion and power systems. 
A dedicated CAN connector is provided to expose the CAN bus externally, supplying: 
• CANH and CANL differential signal lines 
• Ground reference 
• Optional power rail (depending on peripheral requirements) 
The physical-layer implementation is compatible with standard CAN bus topologies and 
supports configurable bit rates within the operating limits of the transceiver and MCU FDCAN 
peripheral. Bus termination is treated as a system-level consideration and is expected to be 
applied externally according to the specific network topology and connected devices. 
By integrating the CAN transceiver onboard while exposing the bus through a standardized 
connector, the design enables modular expansion toward CAN-based peripherals without 
imposing fixed assumptions about network size or device roles. 
2.4.6 Airspeed Sensor Interface 
The flight controller provides a dedicated airspeed sensor connector intended for integration of 
external differential air-pressure sensors commonly used in fixed-wing and VTOL configurations. 
The interface is defined at the electrical and protocol level to allow compatibility with a range of 
commercially available airspeed modules without constraining the specific sensor 
implementation. 
The airspeed connector provides: 
• Regulated 3.3 V supply 
• I²C communication interface (SCL, SDA) 
• Ground reference 
The I²C interface supports standard-mode (100 kHz) and fast-mode (400 kHz) operation, 
consistent with common digital airspeed sensors. Pull-up resistors are implemented on the 
flight controller to satisfy I²C bus electrical requirements, accounting for external sensor 
modules and cable length.

By defining the airspeed interface as a digital I²C connection rather than an analog input, the 
design avoids the need for onboard analog signal conditioning while maintaining flexibility in

2.4.7 Design Standards and Protocols 
The sensor and peripheral subsystem are designed in accordance with established digital 
communication and interface standards. SPI-based devices follow the Motorola SPI 
specification, while I²C-connected sensors adhere to the NXP I²C-bus specification, including 
open-drain signaling and pull-up requirements. Asynchronous serial communication for GPS, 
telemetry, and digital video control interfaces follow UART framing conventions. The CAN 
interface is implemented in accordance with ISO 11898-1 and ISO 11898-2, defining the CAN 
data link and physical layers. Analog video signaling, and airspeed sensor for the onboard OSD 
follows standard composite video conventions. Connector definition and schematic-level 
interface design adhere to IPC-2221 guidelines. Layout-dependent EMC and signal-integrity 
considerations are addressed separately in the PCB layout section.

2.5 External Interfaces and Connector Architecture 
The external interface architecture defines the modular expansion capability of the flight 
controller and establishes clear electrical boundaries between the onboard core subsystems 
and user-selectable external modules. While critical sensing and processing functions are 
implemented through fixed onboard components, all mission-dependent peripherals actuation, 
communication, navigation modules, and video systems are interfaced through dedicated 
connectors with explicitly defined electrical contracts. 
This separation allows the same hardware platform to support multiple UAV configurations 
without schematic modification, while preserving deterministic behavior for time-critical 
onboard subsystems. 
2.5.1 Motor and Servo Control Interfaces 
The flight controller provides a total of 12 independent PWM output channels, all configurable 
at the firmware level and mapped to MCU timer resources. This output capacity is selected to 
support a wide range of UAV configurations, including multirotor, fixed-wing, and VTOL 
platforms, without requiring hardware modification. 
The standard allocation of PWM outputs is organized as follows: 
• 4 PWM outputs dedicated to a 4-in-1 ESC interface, intended for compact multirotor 
configurations using integrated ESC modules 
• 4 PWM outputs exposed as individual motor control pins, supporting discrete ESCs 
commonly used in larger multirotor or hybrid configurations 
• 4 PWM outputs allocated to servo control, enabling actuation of control surfaces, tilt 
mechanisms, or auxiliary devices in fixed-wing and VTOL systems 
All PWM outputs operate at 3.3 V logic levels and are generated using hardware timers to 
ensure deterministic timing and consistent pulse width generation. Output frequency and pulse 
width are configurable through firmware to support standard ESC protocols and servo control 
requirements. 
Servo power is not sourced from the flight controller logic supply. Instead, servo power is 
provided externally through the system stack or a dedicated power-distribution board. This 
design choice allows the use of high-voltage servos and prevents high-current or high-voltage 
actuation loads from coupling into sensitive onboard electronics. The flight controller therefore 
supplies only the control signal and ground reference for servo outputs, while power delivery is 
managed externally according to application requirements.

compatibility with a wider range of actuators, and aligns with modular UAV system

2.5.2 Radio Control Receiver Interface   
A dedicated RC receiver connector supports serial receiver protocols commonly used in UAV 
systems, including SBUS and CRSF (ELRS). The interface provides: 
• Regulated supply voltage 
• UART signal input at 3.3 V logic levels 
• Ground reference 
The receiver interface is electrically isolated from high-bandwidth sensor buses and motor 
outputs, ensuring reliable reception of control data under electrically noisy operating 
conditions. 
2.5.3 Telemetry 
A dedicated telemetry UART interface is exposed through connector pins to support 
bidirectional data exchange with external ground stations, radios, or companion computers. 
This interface operates at 3.3 V logic levels and supports configurable baud rates within the 
UART capabilities of the MCU. 
For development and system bring-up, a standard ST-Link debug connector is provided, 
exposing SWD signals, power reference, and ground. This interface enables low-level firmware 
flashing, debugging, and register-level inspection without interfering with operational 
connectors.

provides:

2.5.5 Connector Reuse and User Responsibility 
Several external connectors on the flight controller are defined by protocol and electrical 
characteristics rather than fixed functional roles. If the electrical constraints and communication 
protocol requirements of a given connector are satisfied, certain interfaces may be repurposed 
to support alternative peripherals. Examples include reassigning UART-based connectors for 
telemetry, control, or peripheral communication, or using I²C connectors for different 
compatible sensor modules. 
This design approach increases hardware flexibility while preserving deterministic electrical 
behavior. Responsibility for ensuring protocol compatibility, voltage requirements, and correct 
peripheral configuration rests with the user when repurposing connectors beyond their typical 
application. The flight controller hardware guarantees defined voltage levels, signal integrity at 
the schematic level, and protocol-compliant electrical interfaces, but does not enforce 
functional constraints on externally connected devices. 
This separation of hardware capability and application-level responsibility allows advanced 
users to adapt the platform to specialized use cases without requiring hardware modification.

2.6 PCB Layout, Stack-Up, Signal Integrity, and EMC Design 
The PCB layout and stack-up are treated as a primary design axis due to their direct impact on 
electromagnetic emissions, susceptibility, and overall system reliability. Given the mixed-signal 
nature of the flight controller combining high-speed digital interfaces, switching power supplies, 
sensor electronics, and high-current actuation paths, the layout is explicitly designed to meet 
EMI/EMC design-for-compliance requirements, rather than relying on post-layout mitigation. 
2.6.1 Six-Layer Stack-Up Architecture 
A six-layer PCB stack-up is employed to provide controlled return paths, effective 
electromagnetic shielding, and low-impedance power distribution. The layer assignment is 
organized as follows: 
• Top layer: Signal routing referenced to an adjacent solid ground plane 
• Layer 2: Power plane (VCC) 
• Layer 3: Solid signal ground reference 
• Layer 4: Segregated power plane for noisy or high-current domains 
• Layer 5: Solid ground plane 
• Bottom layer: Signal routing referenced to an adjacent ground plane 
This ground-power-ground sandwich structure ensures that all high-edge-rate signals are routed 
adjacent to continuous reference planes, minimizing loop area and reducing both radiated and 
conducted emissions. 
2.6.2 EMI-Driven Grounding and Return-Path Control 
Electromagnetic emissions are primarily controlled through return-path integrity rather than 
component-level filtering. Continuous ground planes are maintained across internal layers to 
provide predictable, low impedance return paths for all signal currents. Signal routing is 
constrained such that return currents remain directly beneath their corresponding signal traces, 
avoiding plane discontinuities and unintended current loops. 
Split grounds are intentionally avoided. Instead, noise isolation between sensitive sensor circuits 
and high-energy digital or power domains is achieved through physical separation, controlled 
routing, and plane referencing, which is consistent with modern EMC design practice.

2.6.3 Power Plane Segregation and Noise Containment 
Power planes are separated by function to limit the propagation of switching noise across the 
PCB. High-current and high-di/dt power paths are confined to dedicated internal layers and 
routed to minimize loop area. This approach reduces common-mode noise injection into signal 
reference planes and external cables, a common source of EMI failures. 
Decoupling and bulk capacitors are placed to form localized current loops between power and 
ground planes, further reducing high-frequency emissions. 
2.6.4 EMC and Certification-Oriented Design Standards 
The PCB layout and stack-up are designed in alignment with recognized EMC standards used in 
electronic product certification: 
• IEC 61000-6-3 / IEC 61000-6-4 — Emission standards for residential, commercial, and 
industrial environments 
• IEC 61000-6-1 / IEC 61000-6-2 — Immunity standards for residential and industrial 
environments 
• IEC 61000-4-2 / -4 / -5 — ESD, EFT, and surge immunity test references (design-for-
compliance) 
• CISPR 32 — Radiated and conducted emissions for multimedia and digital equipment 
• IPC-2221 — PCB layout, grounding, and stack-up design guidelines 
These standards guide layout decisions related to ground continuity, return-path control, plane 
usage, and connector placement. While formal EMC certification testing is outside the scope of 
this project, the layout is explicitly designed to maximize the likelihood of first-pass compliance 
under standard EMI/EMC test conditions.

3.3 Test Platform 
System validation and flight testing will be conducted using a 7-inch quadcopter platform, 
selected to provide a stable and representative test environment for evaluating the flight 
controller hardware and firmware. This platform offers sufficient payload capacity to 
accommodate the custom flight controller, external sensors, power distribution hardware, and 
digital video systems, while remaining manageable for iterative testing and tuning. 
The quadcopter configuration will be used to validate core system functionality, including power 
integrity under dynamic load, data acquisition, actuator signal generation, and manual flight 
stabilization. Testing on this platform enables controlled evaluation of real-world operating 
conditions such as vibration, electrical noise from propulsion systems, and environmental 
disturbances, which are critical for assessing the robustness of the hardware design. 
The test platform will be equipped with standard multirotor components, including brushless 
motors, electronic speed controllers (ESCs), a lithium-polymer battery, and an RC receiver, 
providing a representative operating environment for the flight controller. Initial validation will 
focus on bench testing and tethered tests, followed by free flight tests once system stability is 
confirmed. 
If time and system maturity permit, additional validation may be conducted on a fixed-wing or 
VTOL platform to demonstrate the modularity of the hardware architecture and the flexibility of 
the sensor and actuator interfaces. Such testing would focus on reuse of the same flight 
controller hardware with minimal configuration changes, rather than full development of fixed-
wing-specific control algorithms. This staged testing approach prioritizes reliable validation of 
the core hardware platform while allowing optional extension to additional airframe 
configurations if project constraints are allowed.

5. Limitations 
Despite careful planning and engineering-oriented design decisions, the project is subject to 
several practical limitations that influence the scope and validation depth of the final system. 
5.1 Budget Constraints 
The project is developed under a strict budget limitation of approximately 800 GEL (≈ 280 USD). 
This constraint affects component selection, number of prototype iterations, and feature 
inclusions. As a result, the design prioritizes modularity, reuse of commercially available 
components, and a single primary hardware prototype, with limited allowance for multiple PCB 
fabrication or extensive hardware redundancy. 
5.2 EMI/EMC Testing Limitations 
While the PCB layout and stack-up are explicitly designed with EMI/EMC compliance principles 
in mind, formal electromagnetic emission and immunity testing cannot be performed within the 
scope of this project. Certified EMI/EMC validation requires specialized equipment such as 
spectrum analyzers, EMI receivers, LISNs, and shielded test environments, which are not 
accessible under the available budget and laboratory resources. 
To mitigate this limitation, the project adopts a design-for-compliance approach, relying on 
established layout practices and pre-compliance analysis methods. Signal integrity, power 
integrity, and noise coupling are evaluated using simulation and analytical tools, including 
circuit-level simulation (e.g., SPICE-based tools ,Open EMS), interface modeling, and 
manufacturer-provided reference models. Layout decisions are guided by industry standards 
and best practices to reduce the likelihood of EMI/EMC issues prior to any formal certification 
testing. 
5.3 Testing Scope and Validation Depth 
System validation is primarily limited to bench testing and flight testing on representative 
airframes, rather than exhaustive environmental or regulatory testing. As a result, performance 
validation focuses on functional correctness, stability, and robustness under realistic operating 
conditions rather than compliance with formal certification thresholds. 
These limitations are acknowledged as inherent to an academic, prototype-level project and do 
not detract from the technical validity of the design. Instead, they reflect realistic engineering 
trade-offs and reinforce the project’s emphasis on sound architecture, standards-aware design, 
and future extensibility toward more comprehensive testing if resources permit.
